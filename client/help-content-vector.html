Overview

CodeSignal Linear Algebra Playground is a visual learning tool that helps you understand vector operations through hands-on experimentation. Draw vectors on a Cartesian plane and perform operations like addition, subtraction, scalar multiplication, and dot products with animated visualizations.

Controls

  • Click and drag on the grid to create a vector.
  • Use the buttons in the left sidebar to perform mathematical operations on your vectors.
  • Watch the animated result appear on the grid and see the mathematical formulas in the Results section.

Key Features

Vector Addition

Add two vectors together to see the resulting vector. The operation v₁ + v₂ is visualized with smooth animation showing how the vectors combine. The result follows the "tip-to-tail" method commonly taught in physics and mathematics.

Vector Subtraction

Subtract one vector from another (v₁ - v₂). This operation helps visualize the difference between vectors and is useful for understanding relative positions and directions.

Scalar Multiplication

Multiply a vector by a scalar (number) to change its magnitude while preserving or reversing its direction. Enter any decimal value and see how it scales your vector. Negative scalars flip the vector's direction.

Dot Product

Calculate the dot product (v₁ · v₂) between two vectors. This fundamental operation returns a scalar value and is essential for understanding projection, work, and angles between vectors. The formula and step-by-step calculation are displayed.

Vector Projection

Project vector v₁ onto vector v₂ to find the component of v₁ that lies along v₂. This operation is crucial for decomposing vectors into components and understanding how much of one vector "goes in the direction" of another. The result shows proj_v₂(v₁) as a vector along v₂.

Angle Between Vectors

Calculate the angle between two vectors using the dot product formula. This operation shows the geometric relationship between vectors, displaying the result in both degrees and radians. Understanding this angle helps interpret the dot product geometrically.

Unit Vector / Normalization

Convert any vector into a unit vector (length = 1.0) that points in the same direction. Normalization is fundamental in linear algebra for representing pure direction without magnitude. The result shows û₁ or û₂ with coordinates that maintain direction while achieving unit length.

Perpendicular Vector

Generate a vector perpendicular (90° rotation) to an existing vector. This operation demonstrates the geometric relationship between vectors and is useful for understanding orthogonality, normal vectors, and rotations in 2D space.

Real-time Calculations

As you create and modify vectors, the sidebar instantly displays their coordinates, magnitudes (lengths), and angles. All operations show both the mathematical formula and the numerical result.

Animated Operations

All vector operations are animated smoothly, helping you visualize the transformation and understand the geometric interpretation of algebraic operations.

FAQ

Why can I only create two vectors?

This tool is designed to focus on binary operations (operations between two vectors) which are fundamental to learning linear algebra. Limiting to two vectors keeps the interface simple and helps you focus on understanding each operation deeply.

What do the coordinates in brackets mean?

The notation [x, y] represents a vector's components. The first number is the x-component (horizontal), and the second is the y-component (vertical). For example, [3, 2] means 3 units right and 2 units up from the origin.

What does the angle measurement represent?

The angle is measured in degrees from the positive x-axis (pointing right), going counter-clockwise. For example, a vector pointing straight up has an angle of 90°, and one pointing left has an angle of 180° or -180°.

How does scalar multiplication work with negative numbers?

Multiplying a vector by a negative scalar reverses its direction while changing its magnitude. For example, multiplying by -1 creates a vector pointing in the exact opposite direction with the same length. Multiplying by -2 reverses direction and doubles the length.

What does it mean when the dot product is zero?

A dot product of zero indicates that the two vectors are perpendicular (orthogonal) to each other. This is a fundamental property in linear algebra and has many applications in physics, computer graphics, and engineering.

What is vector projection and why is it useful?

Vector projection finds the "shadow" or component of one vector along another vector's direction. It's like asking "how much of vector v₁ goes in the direction of v₂?" This is fundamental in physics (calculating work), computer graphics (lighting calculations), and signal processing.

What's the difference between a vector's angle and the angle between two vectors?

A vector's angle (shown in the sidebar) is measured from the positive x-axis. The "angle between vectors" operation calculates the angle separating two vectors, regardless of their individual angles. For example, vectors at 30° and 150° have an angle of 120° between them.

Why would I normalize a vector?

Normalizing creates a unit vector that preserves direction but has length 1.0. This is useful when you only care about direction (like in 3D graphics for surface normals), when you want to scale something by a consistent amount in a certain direction, or when comparing directions without magnitude influencing the comparison.

How is the perpendicular vector calculated?

The perpendicular vector is found by swapping the x and y components and negating one: if v = [x, y], then v⊥ = [-y, x]. This gives a 90° counterclockwise rotation. You can verify perpendicularity by checking that the dot product of v and v⊥ equals zero.
